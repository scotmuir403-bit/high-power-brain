# Scott's Hook Generator

## When to use this skill

Use this skill whenever Scott needs hooks for TikTok videos, LinkedIn posts, or any content. Trigger phrases include: "give me hooks for", "write me some hooks", "hook ideas for", "I need a hook about", "generate hooks", or any request for a video opener, scroll-stopper, or first line.

---

## Who Scott is (never break this)

Scott Muir. Glasgow. Late 40s. 30 years in highway maintenance, still on night shifts. Building Digital Made Simple — AI content and automation services for small businesses. He is NOT a guru. He's a working bloke figuring it out in real time. His audience respects that. Never break that framing.

---

## Scott's audience

**Primary:** Small business owners and tradesmen (plumbers, electricians, builders, joiners) aged 40–60 who are time-poor, tech-sceptical, and tired of being talked down to.

**Secondary:** Working-class men in their 40s/50s who are curious about AI but don't know where to start.

**What they want:** Save time. Make more money. Feel less overwhelmed. Not be embarrassed in front of younger people for not knowing tech stuff.

**What they fear:** Wasting time on something that doesn't work. Looking stupid. Being sold to. Being patronised.

**What they're tired of hearing:** "Game-changing." "Crushing it." "This went viral." "You just need to post more." "Work smarter not harder." Guru talk. Hustle culture.

**What makes them stop scrolling:** A line that sounds like it was written by someone who actually understands their life — not someone trying to sell them something.

---

## Scott's voice rules

- Short sentences. Plain words. Active voice.
- Peer-to-peer tone — never expert talking down to student
- Dry, self-aware, working-class humour where it fits naturally
- Honest about where Scott actually is — no fake authority
- Sounds like it came out of a Glasgow bloke's mouth, not a marketing textbook

**Tone to avoid (patterns, not a strict word ban):**
The goal is to avoid the pattern these phrases represent — hollow hype, guru positioning, fake urgency. A specific word from this list might work fine in the right context; the pattern is what to avoid: "game-changing" / "crushing it" / "millionaire morning routine" / "secret hack" / "unlock your potential" / "hustle" / "empire building". If the line sounds like it came from a motivational poster or a £997 course ad — rewrite it.

**Language that lands in Scott's world:**
Concrete, specific, plain. "Saved 2 hours" beats "massive time-saver." "Most tradesmen skip this" beats "most people don't know this secret." Words like: honest / real / simple / sorted / wasted / straightforward / actually works / the part nobody tells you / this took me too long to figure out.

## The Hook Pro Framework

### Step 1 — Identify the viewer's hidden tension

Before writing any hook, identify:
- What the viewer **wants** (the outcome)
- What they **fear** (the cost of inaction or the wrong move)
- What they already **believe** (the assumption the hook can challenge or confirm)
- What they **secretly suspect** but haven't acted on
- What they are **tired of hearing**
- What outcome would make them **stop scrolling**

The strongest hook attacks the gap between "what they think is true" and "what they need to hear next."

### Step 2 — Choose hook type(s) from these 11 categories

**1. Curiosity hooks** — create an information gap
Formula: "You think X, but Y is the thing nobody explains."
Works when: the viewer assumes they already know the answer but they don't

**2. Loss-aversion hooks** — make the cost of ignoring feel immediate
Formula: "If you keep doing X, you're losing Y."
Works when: the viewer is leaking time, money, or opportunity right now without knowing it

**3. Contrarian hooks** — challenge common advice
Formula: "Everyone says X. That's why you're stuck."
Works when: there's a piece of advice Scott's audience has heard a hundred times and it isn't working

**4. Identity-based hooks** — speak to who they want to become
Formula: "People like you don't do X — they do Y."
Works when: the viewer has a self-image Scott can confirm or challenge

**5. Emotional-switch hooks** — pair a normal situation with an unexpected emotion
Formula: "You should feel X about this, but actually you should feel Y."
Works when: there's a surprising reframe on something familiar

**6. Storytelling hooks** — open a loop with a specific moment
Formula: "I did X, expected Y, then Z happened."
Works when: Scott has a real example, client result, or personal moment to draw from

**7. Bold statement hooks** — make a sharp claim that demands proof
Formula: "X is not the problem. Y is."
Works when: the viewer is solving the wrong problem

**8. Challenge hooks** — invite the viewer to test themselves
Formula: "Try this and you'll see X."
Works when: the viewer can immediately test what you're saying against their own situation

**9. Future-pacing hooks** — show the viewer a better near future
Formula: "Once you understand X, you'll stop struggling with Y."
Works when: the viewer is stuck in a frustrating loop and can picture escape

**10. Demonstration hooks** — promise visible proof
Formula: "Watch me turn X into Y."
Works when: Scott can show a before/after or live result

**11. Searchable hooks** — use phrases people search for, but add intrigue
Formula: "How to X without Y."
Works when: the topic has obvious search intent from Scott's audience

### Step 3 — Add specificity

Weak: "Here's how to save time with AI."
Stronger: "Most tradesmen spend 90 minutes a day on admin AI could do in 4 minutes."

Specificity (numbers, times, trades, situations) makes the viewer feel the hook was written for them.

### Step 4 — Create a curiosity loop

Every hook should make the viewer ask one of:
- "Why?"
- "How?"
- "What mistake?"
- "Am I doing that?"
- "What happens next?"

If they can answer the hook without watching — rewrite it.

### Step 5 — Add emotional stakes

Each hook should touch one of:
- Fear of wasting effort (done the hard work, got nothing for it)
- Desire for more time (time with family, off the tools)
- Fear of being left behind (younger blokes getting ahead)
- Relief from confusion (it's simpler than they thought)
- Pride (doing something smart, not just hard)
- Frustration with bad advice (finally someone being straight)

### Step 6 — Avoid hollow patterns

Check for language that sounds like a guru wrote it — vague hype, fake urgency, motivational poster energy. Replace with concrete, specific, emotionally honest language that fits Scott's world. The test is the pattern, not the individual word.

### Step 7 — Make the first line self-contained

The viewer should feel the tension in the first line alone — no context needed.

### Step 8 — Test every hook before outputting

Each hook must pass:
1. Does it create curiosity?
2. Does it imply a clear payoff?
3. Does it target a real pain or desire of Scott's audience?
4. Does it sound specific and real — not recycled?
5. Would a tired tradesman scrolling TikTok at 9pm stop for this — or roll his eyes?

If it fails #5, rewrite it.

## Output format

When generating hooks for a topic:

1. Ask Scott for the video topic if not provided
2. Identify the hidden tension for Scott's audience on that topic
3. Generate hooks across **at least 6 categories** — 3–5 hooks per category
4. Label each category clearly
5. At the end, flag your **top 3 picks** with a one-line reason for each

**Format example:**

---
**Topic:** [topic]
**Hidden tension:** [one sentence — what the audience wants vs what's stopping them]

**Curiosity hooks**
1. [hook]
2. [hook]
3. [hook]

**Loss-aversion hooks**
1. [hook]
2. [hook]
3. [hook]

[...continue for all categories...]

**Top 3 picks:**
- #X — [reason it's the strongest for Scott's audience]
- #X — [reason]
- #X — [reason]
---

## Final check before outputting

Read every hook back through this lens: *"Would a tired working dad scrolling TikTok at midnight after a long shift stop for this — or roll his eyes and scroll past?"*

If he'd roll his eyes — rewrite it.
