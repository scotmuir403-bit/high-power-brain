# Journey Frameworks — Emotional Arc Reference

Every script body is built on one of these four frameworks.
These are not content structures — they are emotional journeys.
The viewer doesn't just hear information. They feel a progression.

Match the framework to the content pillar and hook style before writing the body.

---

## FRAMEWORK 1 — PAIN → AWARENESS → SHIFT → EMPOWERMENT

**Best for:** Pillar 1 (AI on a night shift), Pillar 3 (Building in public), Pillar 5 (AI for everyone)
**Tone:** Honest, grounded, warm. The viewer feels understood before they feel inspired.

**The arc:**
- **PAIN** — Start exactly where they are. Name the struggle without dramatising it.
  The pain must be so specific they think "how did he know?"
- **AWARENESS** — Introduce the thing they hadn't seen yet. Not a solution — a realisation.
  "Here's what I noticed when I stopped assuming AI wasn't for me."
- **SHIFT** — The moment the thinking changed. Scott's or the viewer's.
  A single clear pivot — not a list of changes.
- **EMPOWERMENT** — End with capability, not inspiration.
  Not "you can do it!" — "here's the specific first thing that moves you forward."

**Example flow:**
Pain: "Most people in physical jobs never think about this until it's too late."
Awareness: "AI isn't replacing the skill. It's replacing the admin around the skill."
Shift: "Once I saw that — I stopped being afraid of it and started using it."
Empowerment: "Free resource in bio. That's the first step."

## FRAMEWORK 2 — STRUGGLE → REALISATION → TRANSFORMATION → INNOVATION

**Best for:** Pillar 2 (AI tools explained simply), Pillar 5 (AI for everyone)
**Tone:** Discovery-driven. The viewer goes on the same journey Scott went on.

**The arc:**
- **STRUGGLE** — Open in the specific struggle most people have with this topic.
  Make it concrete and recognisable. "I spent three weeks trying to figure out [X]."
- **REALISATION** — The click moment. What suddenly made sense.
  Keep it to one clear insight — the simpler, the better.
- **TRANSFORMATION** — What changed after the realisation. Specific and honest.
  "Now I use it for [specific task] and it saves me [specific time/effort]."
- **INNOVATION** — The new way forward. What's now possible that wasn't before.
  Not hype — just the practical door that opened.

**Example flow:**
Struggle: "I kept reading about AI automation but had no idea where to actually start."
Realisation: "Then I realised — I didn't need to understand how it worked. Just what it could do."
Transformation: "I set up one simple automation. Now my content plan writes itself every week."
Innovation: "That's one hour a week I get back. Every week. For the rest of the time I'm doing this."

## FRAMEWORK 3 — BEFORE → TURNING POINT → AFTER → NEXT STEP

**Best for:** Pillar 3 (Building in public), Pillar 1 (AI on a night shift)
**Tone:** Story-driven. Classic narrative arc with a practical close.

**The arc:**
- **BEFORE** — Where Scott was. Specific circumstances, not vague backstory.
  "A year ago I had no idea how to use AI. I was working nights and had no plan."
- **TURNING POINT** — The specific moment or decision that changed things.
  One clear event or realisation — not a montage of gradual changes.
- **AFTER** — Where things stand now. Honest — not inflated, not undersold.
  Real numbers or real situations only. "I've got my first client in progress right now."
- **NEXT STEP** — What the viewer should do next. Concrete and specific.
  Not "start your journey" — "here's the one thing that moves you forward today."

**Example flow:**
Before: "A year ago I thought AI was for tech people. Not for someone doing highway maintenance."
Turning Point: "I watched someone my age explain how they'd automated their whole content system. In one afternoon."
After: "I'm still on night shifts. But I've got a client in progress and I'm building this every week."
Next Step: "Free resource in bio. That's where I started and where I'd tell anyone to start."

## FRAMEWORK 4 — FEAR → TRUTH → HOPE → ACTION

**Best for:** Pillar 4 (Anti-guru / myth-bust), Pillar 1 (AI on a night shift)
**Tone:** Direct, calm, honest. The most powerful framework for emotional engagement.

**The arc:**
- **FEAR** — Name the fear they won't say out loud. Not hypothetical — the real one.
  Speak it plainly, without drama. They've been carrying it quietly.
- **TRUTH** — The honest truth about that fear. Not reassurance — reality.
  Sometimes the truth is "that fear is partially valid, here's what's actually true."
- **HOPE** — What becomes possible once the truth replaces the fear.
  Not optimism — evidence. Real, specific, Scott's own situation.
- **ACTION** — The one concrete next step. Low barrier. No pressure.
  Always the free resource. Always calm.

**Example flow:**
Fear: "Most people I talk to are scared AI is going to make them irrelevant."
Truth: "That fear is real. But it's aimed at the wrong thing. AI replaces tasks. Not people who learn to use it."
Hope: "I'm 49. I started learning this last year. I've already built something real around it."
Action: "Free resource in bio. Start there. That's it."

## BODY WRITING RULES

- 150–200 words spoken. 60–90 seconds on camera.
- One idea per sentence. Never two.
- The emotional beat lands in the second section (Awareness / Realisation / Turning Point / Truth).
  That's where the script earns the viewer's trust.
- Always include a pause beat before the close. One sentence of silence.
  Write it in as: "[pause — look to camera]"
- The close is never inspirational. It's calm, direct, and specific.
- Pace: slightly slower than feels natural. Calm beats fast every time.
