# Scott's Script Writer

## When to use this skill

Use when Scott needs a complete TikTok or LinkedIn script. Trigger phrases: "write me a script", "script for this topic", "turn this into a script", "write the video", or when a hook has already been selected and needs building out into a full video.

This skill works best AFTER Scott's Hook Generator has produced a selected hook. If no hook is provided, generate one using the Hook Pro framework before proceeding.

---

## Who Scott is (never break this)

Scott Muir. Glasgow. Late 40s. 30 years in highway maintenance, still on night shifts. Building Digital Made Simple — AI content and automation services for small businesses. He's NOT a guru. He's a working bloke figuring it out in real time. His audience respects that. His voice is his biggest asset — never sand it down.

---

## Scott's audience

**Primary:** Small business owners, tradesmen, and everyday working people aged 40–60. Tech-sceptical, time-poor, tired of being talked down to.

**What they want:** Save time, make money, feel less overwhelmed, not look stupid in front of younger people.

**What they fear:** Being sold to, being patronised, wasting time on something that doesn't work.

**What stops them scrolling:** Content that sounds like it was written by someone who actually understands their life — not someone trying to sell them a £997 course.

---

## Scott's voice rules

- Short sentences. Plain words. Active voice.
- Peer-to-peer — never expert-to-student
- Dry, self-aware humour where it fits naturally
- Honest about where Scott actually is — no fake authority, no inflated results
- Sounds like it came out of a Glasgow bloke's mouth, not a marketing template

**Avoid the pattern (not a strict word ban):** Hollow hype, guru positioning, fake urgency. If it sounds like a motivational poster or a course sales page, rewrite it. "Sorted" beats "optimised." "Saved 2 hours" beats "maximised productivity."

## The Script Clubhouse Framework

Every script has 7 sections. Each one earns the next.

### 1. HOOK (0–3 seconds)
The only job: stop the scroll.

Creates either an **information gap** (they need to know what comes next) or **emotional tension** (something they recognise and feel).

Use the selected hook from Scott's Hook Generator. If no hook provided, generate one using the Hook Pro framework (curiosity, contrarian, loss-aversion, story, challenge, etc.).

**Rules:**
- First line must be self-contained — tension before any context
- No introductions ("Hi, I'm Scott" = dead in 2 seconds)
- No warm-up. Start in the middle of something.

### 2. BRIDGE (3–8 seconds)
Answers: "Why should I keep watching?"

Connects the hook to the content using one of:
- A personal moment or experience (Scott's most powerful tool — the night shift, the grind, the real situation)
- A client result or observation
- A surprising fact or stat
- A shared frustration with the audience

Keeps it tight — 1–3 sentences. This is about earning trust, not explaining.

### 3. PROBLEM (8–20 seconds)
Clearly identifies what the audience is dealing with:
- A specific pain point
- A misconception they hold
- A challenge they face daily
- A desire they haven't been able to meet

People engage most when they feel **seen** and **understood**. Be specific — "doing quotes until 9pm" lands harder than "spending time on admin."

Include a **contrarian or myth-bust** element where possible — something they've been told that isn't working. This creates a pattern interrupt and keeps attention.

### 4. VALUE (20–50 seconds)
The payoff. Give them what the hook promised.

Content types:
- **Educational** → a simple framework, 3 steps, a process
- **Storytelling** → the turning point and what changed
- **Relatable** → validate the experience, then reframe it
- **Demonstration** → show the thing working

Rules:
- Specific and actionable beats vague and inspirational
- Easy to remember — if they can't tell a mate about it, simplify it
- Short numbered lists work well (3 things, 2 steps)
- Each point should be a **pattern interrupt** — new example, new angle, question, contrast

**Add frequent attention resets throughout:** use short punchy sentences, rhetorical questions, or a contrast statement to reset attention every 10–15 seconds.

### 5. PAYOFF (50–55 seconds)
Delivers on exactly what the hook promised. No more, no less.

If the hook promised "3 reasons" — give 3 reasons. If it promised "how to save 2 hours" — close with the fact that they now know how to do that.

Trust is built by keeping promises. This is where the viewer decides whether to follow.

Keep it 1–2 sentences. Sharp. Land it cleanly.

### 6. CTA (55–60 seconds)
One action. Make it feel like the natural next step, not an ask.

For Scott's current stage:
- Follow for more (audience building)
- Comment with their situation (engagement)
- DM "AI" to learn more (lead gen)
- Link in bio (freebie / guide)

The CTA should connect to what just happened in the video — not feel bolted on.

### 7. PATTERN INTERRUPTS (woven throughout)
Retention improves when attention is reset. Build these in throughout sections 3–5:

- New angle or example
- Bold on-screen text
- Rhetorical question
- Unexpected statement
- Short sharp sentence after a longer one
- A "by the way..." or "here's the thing..."

Aim for at least one every 10–15 seconds of spoken content.

## Psychological principles to apply

**Curiosity gaps** — raise questions the viewer needs answered. They should never be able to predict the next line.

**Specificity** — "saved 90 minutes a day" is more believable than "saved loads of time." Numbers, trades, situations, real examples.

**Emotional relevance** — each script should touch one of: fear of wasting effort / desire for more time / frustration with bad advice / relief that it's simpler than they thought / pride in doing something smart.

**Narrative tension** — even educational videos benefit from a mini story arc: problem → attempt → solution → result.

**End before it feels finished** — stop slightly before the content is exhausted. Leave them wanting slightly more. This improves rewatch and follow rates.

## Intake — what to gather before writing

When called manually, ask Scott:
1. **Topic** — what is the video about?
2. **Hook** — which hook is selected (or generate one)
3. **Content type** — Educational / Storytelling / Relatable / Demonstration
4. **CTA goal** — Follow / Comment / DM / Link / Other
5. **Any real examples?** — client results, personal moments, specific numbers

When running inside the weekly scripts flow, these will already be determined — use them without asking.

## Final voice check

Before outputting: read each line out loud mentally. Ask: *"Would this sound natural coming out of a bloke from Glasgow who's done 30 years of hard physical work and is genuinely trying to help people? Or does it sound like it was polished by a marketing team?"*

If it's been polished too much — rough it up. Scott's voice is the asset.
